How does Cornell-style learning work?

- GDucpm -

What I call Cornell-style learning is actually Cornell notes. It's unreasonable that people call it that way, because that means it's only a note organizing convention. The Cornell learning system includes both an organization specification and a 6-step learning process. Let's dive in.

Cornell Notes separates your paper into four partitions. The first partition is a small, horizontal-oriented box – the title. It spans the whole width of your paper, but barely occupies any of the height. The second partition, height-oriented, takes up 1/3 of your paper's width, but vertically leaves some space at the end for a summary. This is the Cues partition, and it sits right below the title and at the very left of your paper. The third partition has the same height, and it fills up the remaining width. It also sits right below the title – the Notes partition. You allocate all the remaining space as the Summary partition.

Step 1: Record your notes at school

At school, note everything you've got into the Notes partition. Try to keep it simple; the limitation of Cornell-style noting is that the partitioning makes it very limited in space. Abbreviate words (only if you can understand it), and if necessary, don't include examples. However, with GTKornell, you can note as long as you want.

Step 2: Record keywords or concepts

That's literally it. Record them into the Cues partition. Additionally, write possible questions that you wonder or might help you upon revision. You will need them.
